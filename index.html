Stop 1 · Gallery 825 · Floor 2

Self-Portrait with a Straw Hat

Vincent van Gogh · 1887 · Oil on canvas
How we read faces
The Museum
Van Gogh produced more than twenty self-portraits during his Parisian sojourn. Short of funds but determined to hone his skills as a figure painter, he became his own best sitter: "I purposely bought a good enough mirror to work from myself, for want of a model."
Kandel
The artist tries not only to capture the essence of a person but also to amplify it by appropriate exaggeration, and thus to activate more powerfully the same neural mechanisms that would have been triggered by the person in real life.
Contrast is useful for face detection, and shape is useful for face recognition.
The artist's ability to abstract the essential features of an image and to discard redundant information is similar to what the visual system has evolved to do.
The Library
How Minds Change · David McRaneyNo matter the message, face-to-face messaging is far and away the most effective channel. We are biologically hardwired to respond to the human face.
Daring Greatly · Brené BrownLet your face speak what's in your heart. When they walk in the room, my face says I'm glad to see them. It's just as small as that, you see?

Stop 2 · Gallery 625 · Floor 2

Juan de Pareja

Velázquez · 1650 · Oil on canvas
Whose face do we see?
The Museum
Velázquez traveled to Italy with Juan de Pareja, a man of African descent enslaved in his studio for at least two decades. He exhibited this portrait in Rome, "which was so like him and so lively that they just stood looking at the portrait in admiration and wonder, not knowing to whom they should speak or who would answer."
Kandel
Faces are essential for our social interaction.
We communicate not only our ideas and plans but also our emotions through our faces.
A powerful link between the face-detection machinery and the areas that control attention, which may account for why faces and portraiture draw our attention so strongly.
Theory of mind is the idea that someone else has a mind of his or her own, with separate feelings, aspirations, and ideas.
The Library
Doppelganger · Naomi KleinFor a great many people, the face's ability to communicate is sabotaged by other people's warped receptors all the time.
Seven and a Half Lessons About the Brain · Lisa Feldman BarrettBabies are often not exposed to a wide array of facial features. That means the baby's brain does not tune itself to detect those different features.
Caste · Isabel WilkersonColor is a fact. Race is a social construct.

Stop 3 · Gallery 115 · Floor 1

Seated Statue of Hatshepsut

New Kingdom, Dynasty 18 · c. 1479–1458 BC · Limestone, paint
When the face doesn't compute
The Museum
Hatshepsut declared herself king. In this life-size statue, she wears the ceremonial attire of the Egyptian king — traditionally a man's role. In spite of the masculine dress, the statue has a distinctly feminine air. The kingly titles are feminized to read "the Perfect Goddess, Lady of the Two Lands."
Kandel
Face recognition is uniquely sensitive to inversion: not only do we have difficulty recognizing a face upside down, we cannot recognize a change in expression on an inverted face.
Every powerful image is inherently ambiguous because it arises from experiences and conflicts in the artist's life.
The extent of the viewer's contribution depends on the degree of ambiguity in the image.
The Library
How Emotions Are Made · Lisa Feldman BarrettYou are not finding similarities in the world but creating them. When your brain needs a concept, it constructs one on the fly.
How Emotions Are Made · Lisa Feldman BarrettYour brain also magnifies differences between members and nonmembers.

How Minds Change · David McRaneyThe image never seemed ambiguous because consciously people experienced only the output of their processes. The result was a lie told to them by their brains that felt obviously true.

Stop 4 · Gallery 548 · Floor 1

Perseus with the Head of Medusa

Antonio Canova · 1804–6 · Marble
Bodies that speak
The Museum
Perseus triumphantly presents Medusa's head to the world. The Gorgon Medusa was feared for her ability to turn living men to stone. Here, Canova inverts this process. Deploying his renowned skill for manipulating the surface of marble, he has turned cold stone into warm flesh.
Kandel
Sculpture calls into play more powerful tactile and kinesthetic sensations than paintings do. Even if we do not actually touch a sculpture, it elicits from us both a desire to touch it and the sensation of touching it.
Sculpture achieves this by recruiting several of the brain mechanisms used for social interaction.
Sculpture elicits a more pronounced desire to simulate and empathize with a motion being depicted than drawings or paintings do.
The Library
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulAt a deep level the brain still understands abstract concepts in terms of physical action — 'reaching for a goal,' 'running behind schedule.' We can assist the brain by bringing the literal body back into the act of thinking.
The Body Keeps the Score · Bessel van der KolkEven when we are by ourselves, our own muscles tensing as the players run and jump… most of our energy is devoted to connecting with others.

Stop 5 · Gallery 548 · Floor 1

Winter

Jean Antoine Houdon · 1787 · Bronze
Touch as truth
The Museum
A frileuse is a woman subject to cold. For this bronze, cast by Houdon himself, he stripped the spiraling columnar composition to its bare essentials. The girl's tremulous flesh is offset by her tightly drawn shawl, elegant but hardly adequate.
Kandel
Touch is the most faithful and fundamental of the five senses. It is the foundation and guarantor of sight: 'in the sense of sight is dream, in the sense of touch, truth.'
The texture of an object activates neurons in these regions, whether the object is perceived by the eye or the hand.
This is why we can easily identify and distinguish between the different materials of an object — skin, cloth, wood, or metal — and can do so at a glance.
A powerful example of the brain's ability to process touch and vision together into a multisensory perception.
The Library
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulWhen we begin using a tool, our 'body schema' rapidly expands to encompass it, as if the tool has effectively become an extension of our arm.
Stealing Fire · Steven Kotler & Jamie WhealWe're not smart and we have bodies — we're smart because we have bodies.

Stop 6 · Gallery 536 · Floor 1

Spinario

Antico · c. 1496/1501 · Bronze, gilt, silver
The classical body
The Museum
A boy pulling a thorn from his foot. Antico subtly animates the figure, conveying the boy's tension as he performs his tricky — and potentially painful — task. We become witnesses to two acts of concentration: the youth's, and the artist's.
Kandel
We actually perceive sculpture differently than we do paintings.
Because of sculpture's three-dimensionality, we have a more visceral reaction to its qualities — delicate or aggressive, flowing or taut, relaxed or dynamic.
We identify much more strongly with the bodily presence and intense focus of the boy than with the vague gaze of Leonardo's lady.
The Library
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulLinking movement to the material to be recalled creates a richer and more indelible 'memory trace' in the brain — a phenomenon researchers call the 'enactment effect.'

Stop 7 · Gallery 548 · Floor 1

The Burghers of Calais

Auguste Rodin · modeled 1884–95 · Bronze, life-size
Empathy in three dimensions
The Museum
Six leading citizens offered their lives to the English king in exchange for the lifting of his siege. By portraying their despair and haunted courage in the face of death, Rodin challenged contemporary heroic ideals and made an event from the past seem immediate and real.
Kandel
Art is to enable us to experience aspects of life we would not normally experience. One way to do that is through empathy, which is strongly represented in the brain.
Much as mirror neurons may be used to interpret the meaning of observed behaviors, we can almost see the actions of the artist's hand in the traces on the canvas.
The default mode network comes online for the highest aesthetic experiences: it is stimulated when we encounter artworks that resonate with our sense of self.
The Library
The Body Keeps the Score · Bessel van der KolkEven when we are by ourselves, our own muscles tensing… most of our energy is devoted to connecting with others.
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulHuman thought is exquisitely sensitive to context, and one of the most powerful contexts of all is the presence of other people.
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulWhen we listen to a story, our brains experience the action as if it were happening to us. When we hear about characters moving vigorously, the motor regions of our brains are roused.
Seven and a Half Lessons · Lisa Feldman BarrettA mind is something that emerges from a transaction between your brain and your body while they are surrounded by other brains-in-bodies constructing a social world.

Stop 8 · Gallery 614 · Floor 2

A Maid Asleep

Johannes Vermeer · c. 1656–57 · Oil on canvas
What you add to the picture
The Museum
Vermeer transfigured an ordinary scene into an investigation of light, color, and texture. X-radiographs indicate that Vermeer chose to remove a male figure he had originally included standing in the doorway, heightening the painting's ambiguity.
Kandel
The beholder's share — the knowledge, experience, and memories a viewer brings to a work of art — can create very different conceptions of an ambiguous image.
The viewer responds to this ambiguity in terms of his or her own experiences and conflicts.
The more ambiguous a work of art, the more top-down processing we must do to resolve the ambiguities and assign the work meaning, utility, and value.
We are not normally aware of constructing visual hypotheses and drawing conclusions.
The Library
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulEarly on in the process, vagueness and ambiguity are more generative than explicitness or definition.
How Minds Change · David McRaneyFor narrative transport to take place, a story must contain three features: a component that keeps your attention from wandering, one that evokes strong emotional reactions, and one that evokes mental imagery.
How to Change Your Mind · Michael PollanOnce we register on our retinas the visual pattern of the object we're searching for, it's much more likely to pop out of the visual field.

Stop 9 · Gallery 826 · Floor 2

The Card Players

Paul Cézanne · 1890–92 · Oil on canvas
Seeing what isn't there
The Museum
Cézanne carefully crafted this composition from figure studies of local farmhands. He continued to fine-tune the poses and positions of the card players, until they — like the four pipes hanging on the wall behind them — each fell perfectly into place.
Kandel
Helmholtz realized that the images our eyes transmit to our brain contain poor-quality, incomplete information.
Our brain is not simply a camera; it is a creativity machine. It takes incomplete information from the outside world and makes it complete.
There is no 'innocent eye': all visual perception is based on classifying concepts and interpreting visual information.
Cubism dares our visual system to reconstruct an image fundamentally different from the kinds of images our brain evolved to reconstruct.
The Library
An Immense World · Ed YongColor is fundamentally subjective. Our brains use signals from photoreceptors to construct the sensation of color.
How Minds Change · David McRaneyThere are more than thirty steps in visual processing before an image reaches consciousness. You are only aware of the result, not the processes.
Originals · Adam GrantPicasso's oeuvre includes more than 1,800 paintings, 1,200 sculptures, 2,800 ceramics, and 12,000 drawings — only a fraction of which have garnered acclaim.

Stop 10 · Gallery 815 · Floor 2

The Little Fourteen-Year-Old Dancer

Edgar Degas · c. 1878–81 · Bronze, cotton, silk, wood
Where knowing becomes feeling
The Museum
The pubescent dancer's jaunty, almost defiant pose and real clothing made her uncomfortably lifelike. Huysmans wrote, "The terrible realism of this statuette makes the public distinctly uneasy, all its ideas about sculpture, about cold lifeless whiteness, are demolished."
Kandel
This multisensory representation is the key to how we experience art.
An individual's taste in art is linked to his or her sense of identity.
The brain generates internal representations of what we see in the world around us, whether as an artist or as a beholder.
The Library
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulThinking outside the brain means skillfully engaging the feelings and movements of our bodies, the physical spaces in which we learn, and the minds of other people around us.
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulIntelligence is not 'a fixed lump of something that's in our heads.' It's a transaction: a fluid interaction among our brains, our bodies, our spaces, and our relationships.
The Extended Mind · Annie Murphy PaulWe will find ourselves needing to translate abstractions back into the corporeal, spatial, and social forms from which they sprang — forms with which the brain is still most at ease.
Co-Intelligence · Ethan MollickWe need connected information, and lots of it, to be stored in our long-term memory. And that means we need to learn many facts and understand how they are connected.

You've walked through 3,500 years of human art. At every stop, your brain was doing exactly what Kandel describes — completing, simulating, empathizing, constructing. The art hasn't changed since you walked in. But the way you see it has.
