Juan de Pareja

Velázquez · 1650 · Oil on canvas
Whose face do we see?
The Museum
Velázquez traveled to Italy with Juan de Pareja, a man of African descent enslaved in his studio for at least two decades. He exhibited this portrait in Rome, where onlookers could not tell the portrait from the living person. The Met paid five and a half million dollars for this painting in 1971 — then a world record.
Kandel
Faces are essential for our social interaction.
Of all the objects in the world, faces are the most special from a biological point of view.
As Charles Darwin first pointed out, we recognize each other and even ourselves through our faces.
Moreover, since we are social animals, we communicate not only our ideas and plans but also our emotions through our faces.
The ability to form facial expressions and to read those of others is innate, not learned, and our brains treat faces differently from all other objects.
A powerful link between the face-detection machinery and the areas that control attention, which may account for why faces and portraiture draw our attention so strongly.

Stop 4 · Gallery 111 · Floor 1

Portrait of the Boy Eutyches

Roman Egypt · c. 100–150 AD · Encaustic on wood
The face machine
The Museum
This portrait was painted nearly two thousand years ago using hot wax mixed with pigment pressed into a wooden panel — a technique called encaustic. It was placed over the face of a mummified body. An inscription identifies the subject as Eutyches, a freedman. These are among the earliest naturalistic portraits to survive from the ancient world — painted to accompany the dead, but staring back at the living with an immediacy that has not dimmed in two millennia.
Kandel
The brain treats faces very differently from other objects.
If we turn a bottle of water upside down, we will still recognize it as a bottle of water. But we may not recognize a face when it is upside down.
The preference for a particular facial feature depends on its luminance relative to other parts of the face.
Most of the cells in the middle face patches respond both to contrast and to the shape of facial features.
Contrast is useful for face detection, and shape is useful for face recognition.
Face recognition is uniquely sensitive to inversion: not only do we have difficulty recognizing a face upside down, we cannot recognize a change in expression on an inverted face.

Water-Moon Guanyin

Chinese, Liao dynasty · 11th century · Wood (willow) with traces of pigment
Bodies that speak
The Museum
This bodhisattva, carved from willow wood nearly a thousand years ago, sits in a posture known as royal ease. His craggy seat represents Potalaka, an island paradise in the southern sea. His eyes are downcast and his lips gently pursed, giving the slightest hint of a smile — a figure that occupies a liminal space between the ideal and the real, the divine and the mortal.
Kandel
We actually perceive sculpture differently than we do paintings.
Sculpture calls into play more powerful tactile and kinesthetic sensations than paintings do. Even if we do not actually touch a sculpture, it elicits from us both a desire to touch it and the sensation of touching it.
Because of sculpture's three-dimensional form, we have a more visceral reaction to its qualities — delicate or aggressive, flowing or taut, relaxed or dynamic.
Sculpture achieves this by recruiting several of the brain mechanisms used for social interaction.
Sculpture also invites tactile sensitivity. It elicits a more pronounced desire to simulate and empathize with a motion being depicted than drawings or paintings do.
Art is to enable us to experience aspects of life we would not normally experience. One way to do that is through empathy, which is strongly represented in the brain.

Kandel
The lack of objects in abstract art requires the viewer to create new personal associations.
When we view figurative art, our eye movements are narrowly distributed and object-focused, whereas when we view abstract art, our viewing patterns are more globally distributed.
Viewing representational art activates areas that respond to faces, places, or objects, whereas abstract art activates areas that respond to features of intermediate complexity, such as simple shape and color.
Abstract art elicits more psychological distance than representational art and thus evokes a more abstract cognitive state in the beholder.
Actively construing abstract art transcends direct sensory experience and evokes higher-level representations in the beholder, potentially activating regions of the brain involved in memory.
Abstract art enables the beholder to traverse mental time and space, resulting in a more distant temporal and spatial placement in the world.

Stop 9 · Lauder Collection · Floor 2

Cubist Works

Picasso, Braque, Gris, Léger · The Lauder Collection
Art that breaks your visual system
The Museum
Picasso and Braque did not destroy form out of carelessness — they dismantled it systematically, testing how far they could push fragmentation before the image collapsed entirely. Every shard of recognizable shape you find — a guitar string, the curve of a shoulder, the rim of a glass — is a lifeline your brain seizes on, desperate to reconstruct a world from pieces.
Kandel
Cubism made the beholder's share the essential subject of art.
Picasso's intuitive insight into the richness of the unconscious mind encouraged Cubists to show the various aspects of one object in the same painting.
Cubism poses such an enormous challenge to the beholder because it dares our visual system to reconstruct an image fundamentally different from the kinds of images our brain evolved to reconstruct.
Cubist art requires the brain to either come up with a new logic of bottom-up processing or to override it altogether by means of top-down processing.
Our bottom-up processes, which follow built-in rules of perspective and Gestalt, cannot help us, and our top-down mental processes must work extra hard to extract meaning.
Such works call for new rules of bottom-up processing that often do not exist and have to be supplied by extensive, creative, top-down processing.
